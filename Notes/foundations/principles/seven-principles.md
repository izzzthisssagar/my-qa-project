# The seven testing principles

*The ISTQB's seven principles distilled — the timeless truths that keep testing effort honest and focused.*

Seven principles underpin all good testing practice:

1. **Testing shows the presence of defects, not their absence.** Passing tests
   reduce risk; they never *prove* the software is bug-free.
2. **Exhaustive testing is impossible.** You cannot test every input and path —
   use risk and priorities to focus effort.
3. **Early testing saves time and money.** Start testing activities (even just
   reviewing requirements) as early as possible.
4. **Defects cluster together.** A small number of modules usually contain most
   defects — follow the smoke.
5. **Beware the pesticide paradox.** Repeating the same tests stops finding new
   bugs; review and revise your tests regularly.
6. **Testing is context-dependent.** A pacemaker and a marketing site demand very
   different testing — there is no one-size-fits-all.
7. **Absence-of-errors is a fallacy.** Software that is defect-free but doesn't
   meet users' needs is still a failure.

## Applying them

These aren't trivia for an exam — they are a checklist against your own biases.
When you feel the urge to "test everything," principle 2 pulls you back to risk.
When your suite goes green for weeks, principle 5 asks whether it has gone stale.
