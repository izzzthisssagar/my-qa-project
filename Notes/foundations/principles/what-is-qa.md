# What is QA?

*Quality Assurance is the discipline of preventing defects, not just finding them — a mindset that spans the whole software lifecycle.*

**Quality Assurance (QA)** is the practice of ensuring software meets its
requirements and serves its users well. It is often confused with *testing*, but
the two are not the same:

- **QA** is *process-oriented* and *preventive* — it shapes how software is built
  so defects are less likely in the first place (reviews, standards, definitions
  of done, CI gates).
- **QC (Quality Control)** is *product-oriented* and *reactive* — it inspects the
  built product to find defects (running tests, exploratory sessions).
- **Testing** is one activity within QC — executing the software to observe
  behaviour and compare it against expectations.

## Why it matters

A defect caught in requirements costs a fraction of one caught in production.
QA's job is to shift discovery **left** — earlier in the lifecycle — where fixes
are cheap and cascading damage is small.

## The QA mindset

Good testers are professionally skeptical. They ask "what could go wrong?",
value **the user's experience over the happy path**, and treat every assumption
as a hypothesis to be checked. Quality is a team responsibility; QA makes it
*visible and systematic*.
